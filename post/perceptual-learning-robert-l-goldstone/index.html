Definition of Perceptual Learning

Relatively long-lasting changes to an organism's perceptual system that improve its ability to respond to its environment.

Four mechanisms of perceptual learning

  1. Attention weighting: perception becomes adapted to tasks and environments by increasing attention to important dimensions and features.
  2. Imprinting: receptors are developed that are specialized for stimuli or parts of stimuli.
  3. Differentiation: stimuli that were once indistinguishable become psychologically separated.
  4. Unitization: tasks that originally required the detection of several parts are accomplished by detecting a single constructed unit representing a complex configuration.

Challenge for Definition

Distinguish between perceptual learning and higher-level cognitive learning.

  • Categorical Perception

A phenomenon for attentional accounts of perceptual adaption. People can distinguish between physically different stimuli when the stimuli come from different categories than when they come from the same category.

  • Whole stimulus storage

Imprinting may occur for entire stimuli, where a receptor develops that internalizes specific instances.

  • Feature Imprinting

There is also evidence that people imprint on parts or features of a stimulus. If a stimulus part is important, varies independently of other parts, or occurs frequently, people may develop a specialized detector for that part. - building block

  • Topological Imprinting

It occurs at a more abstract level. Environmental regularities that span across a set of stimuli can also be internalized. It occurs when the space and positions of patterns within the space are learned due to training with patterns.
